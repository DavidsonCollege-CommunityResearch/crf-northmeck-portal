How much does someone need to earn per hour to afford rent without being cost-burdened?

The "housing wage" is the hourly pay a full-time worker needs so rent does not exceed 30% of income. No common local occupation reaches it.

Housing Wage vs. Most Common Local Occupations
Hourly wages for Charlotte-area occupations vs. the housing wage needed to afford a 2-bedroom without being cost-burdened
At $35.08/hr, the housing wage is out of reach for nearly every common occupation in the Charlotte area. Only registered nurses ($40.42/hr) earn enough — most workers in retail, food service, and logistics earn less than half the housing wage.
Source: NLIHC Out of Reach 2024 · BLS Occupational Employment & Wages, Charlotte-Concord-Gastonia MSA 2023
Fair Market Rent by Bedroom Size (2025)
HUD Fair Market Rents for Charlotte vs. Raleigh and Atlanta — how local rents compare across metro peers
Charlotte's Fair Market Rents are broadly comparable to Atlanta's and below Raleigh's for smaller units, but the gap closes — and reverses for 4-bedroom units — as family size grows. A 4-bedroom unit in Charlotte runs $2,852/month, $309 above Atlanta.
Source: HUD FY2025 Fair Market Rents

Who can afford to live in North Meck at different income levels?

Income limits and maximum affordable rent at each AMI bracket compared to fair market rent, showing the monthly gap households face at each income tier.

Affordable Rent vs. Fair Market Rent by AMI Bracket
Max affordable rent (30% of income rule) vs. HUD Fair Market Rent across bedroom sizes — Charlotte-Mecklenburg metro, FY2025
Households earning 30% or 50% of AMI face a severe monthly shortfall — they can afford far less than market rents require. Even at 80% AMI, most bedroom sizes remain out of reach. Only households at 100% AMI can afford the fair market rent for all unit sizes.
Source: HUD FY2025 Income Limits & Fair Market Rents · Charlotte-Concord-Gastonia HUD Metro FMR Area

How does rent — and homeownership — compare to what residents actually earn?

Rent-to-income ratio measures what share of median household income goes to rent. A home price-to-income ratio above 4× is considered unaffordable for first-time buyers without significant savings. Both ratios have climbed sharply since 2018.

Town:
In all three towns, rent now consumes a larger share of household income than it did in 2018. The dashed line marks the 30% cost-burden threshold — once crossed, households have less to spend on food, healthcare, and savings.
Source: ACS 2018–2024 · ratio = (median annual rent) ÷ (median household income)

How many North Meck households are financially vulnerable but invisible to traditional poverty measures?

The Federal Poverty Level (FPL) was designed in the 1960s and does not account for regional cost differences. In North Meck, where costs are high, the FPL misses a large share of struggling households. The ALICE framework (Asset Limited, Income Constrained, Employed) fills this gap.

Income & Need

What do residents earn, and does it reflect true financial vulnerability in North Meck?

Standard poverty measures miss a significant share of struggling households here. This tab explains why, and shows what the data actually reveals.

Why we show ALICE alongside traditional poverty data: The Federal Poverty Level (FPL) was calculated in the 1960s based on food costs and hasn't kept pace with housing, childcare, or healthcare. In wealthy suburbs like North Meck, it makes need nearly invisible. ALICE (Asset Limited, Income Constrained, Employed) measures households that earn above the FPL but still can't cover basic costs — the households who need services at places like the Ada Jenkins Center in Davidson West.
This is a methodological transparency note, not a political statement. Every data choice on this portal is documented.

Glossary

Plain definitions for the terms used across the portal.

No terms match your search.
ALICE (Asset Limited, Income Constrained, Employed)
A United Way framework that identifies households earning above the federal poverty line but below the basic cost of living for their county — meaning they are employed and not "poor" by official measures, yet cannot afford housing, food, childcare, transportation, healthcare, and a smartphone plan simultaneously. In North Meck, ALICE households are the hidden gap between prosperity and poverty.
United Way ALICE Project
ALICE Threshold
The minimum income a household needs to cover a bare-bones budget for housing, food, childcare, transportation, healthcare, and technology in a given county. It is calculated by United Way for every county in the U.S. and is consistently higher than the federal poverty line — in Mecklenburg County it is roughly 2–3× the FPL for a family of four.
United Way ALICE Project
American Community Survey (ACS)
The U.S. Census Bureau's largest ongoing survey, producing yearly estimates of income, rent, housing, and demographics for communities nationwide. Most figures on this site come from the ACS.
U.S. Census Bureau
Area Median Income (AMI)
The midpoint income for households in a metro area, set annually by HUD. In Charlotte-Mecklenburg the 2025 median is $112,200 for a family of four. Housing programs use percentages of AMI (30%, 50%, 80%) to set eligibility.
HUD FY25 Income Limits
Cost-burdened
A household spending more than 30% of its gross monthly income on housing (rent or mortgage plus utilities). At this level, households often struggle to cover food, healthcare, and other essentials.
HUD definition
Data equity
An approach to collecting, analyzing, and presenting data that actively includes marginalized communities and corrects for historical bias. The CRF program applies a data-equity framework to all of its research.
Urban Institute Data Equity Framework, 2021
Eviction filing
A legal document a landlord files to remove a tenant. It appears on a tenant's record even if the case is later dismissed, making future housing harder to secure. In FY25, 52,625 eviction cases were filed in Mecklenburg County.
Mecklenburg County FY25
Fair Market Rent (FMR)
HUD's estimate of the gross rent for a modestly priced unit in a metro area. FY25 Charlotte-Meck: one-bedroom $1,647/month, two-bedroom $1,824/month.
HUD FY25 FMR, Charlotte-Concord-Gastonia MSA
Federal Poverty Line (FPL)
The federal government's official income threshold below which a household is classified as living in poverty. For 2024 it is about $31,200 for a family of four. Critics argue the FPL was designed in the 1960s based on food costs alone and dramatically understates the true cost of living in high-cost suburbs like North Mecklenburg.
U.S. Dept. of Health & Human Services
Gini Coefficient
A statistical measure of income inequality within a population. It ranges from 0 (perfect equality — everyone earns the same) to 1 (maximum inequality — one person earns everything). The U.S. national average is roughly 0.40. Cornelius registered 0.52–0.53 in recent years, approaching levels seen in large cities and indicating a wide gap between its highest- and lowest-income residents.
U.S. Census Bureau ACS
Homeownership rate
The share of households that own, rather than rent, their home. Differences in homeownership by race are a core measure of housing equity and of wealth built up over generations.
U.S. Census ACS
Housing Choice Voucher (Section 8)
A federal program that helps low-income households afford private-market rent. The household generally pays about 30% of its income toward rent and the voucher covers the rest.
HUD
Housing wage
The hourly pay a full-time worker needs to afford a modest rental home without spending more than 30% of their income on rent. For the Charlotte–Mecklenburg metro in 2025, the one-bedroom housing wage is $31.67 an hour.
NLIHC Out of Reach 2025
HUD
The U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development, the federal agency that funds housing assistance and sets the income limits, Fair Market Rents, and affordability standards used throughout this portal.
U.S. Dept. of Housing & Urban Development
Metro area (MSA)
A Metropolitan Statistical Area, a Census-defined region made up of a city and the surrounding communities tied to it economically. Regional figures here use the Charlotte–Concord–Gastonia MSA.
U.S. Census Bureau
Naturally Occurring Affordable Housing (NOAH)
Privately owned, unsubsidized rentals that are affordable because of their age or condition, not a government program. In Mecklenburg County, low-cost rentals (under $800/month) fell from 36% to just 8% of all rental stock between 2015 and 2024.
Charlotte-Mecklenburg State of Housing, 2025
Redlining
A discriminatory practice, widespread from the 1930s onward, in which banks and the government withheld mortgages and investment from neighborhoods because of their racial makeup. Its effects on homeownership and wealth are still visible today.
Mapping Inequality, University of Richmond
Severely cost-burdened
Spending more than 50% of gross monthly income on housing, leaving very little for anything else and sharply raising the risk of eviction or homelessness.
HUD definition
Survival budget
United Way's term for the bare-minimum household spending plan used to set the ALICE Threshold. It includes only non-negotiable monthly costs — a modest apartment, groceries, childcare, a car, basic healthcare, and a smartphone — with nothing left for savings, debt repayment, or emergencies.
United Way ALICE Project
Two-generation model
A service approach that supports children and the adults in their lives at the same time, pairing early education with job training, food assistance, and financial coaching, so a whole family can move toward stability together.
Ada Jenkins Center
