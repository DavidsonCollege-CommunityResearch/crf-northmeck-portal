<p class="post-lead">Ask anyone renting in Cornelius, Davidson, or Huntersville and you will hear a version of the same story: the rent keeps climbing, and the paycheck does not. The numbers back them up and they are starker than most people expect.</p>

<h2>The number that started it</h2>

<p>Every year the National Low Income Housing Coalition calculates a "housing wage," which is the hourly pay a full-time worker needs to afford a modest home without spending more than 30% of their income on rent. For the Charlotte–Mecklenburg metro in 2025, the one-bedroom housing wage is <strong>$31.67 an hour</strong>.</p>

<p>That is more than four times the federal minimum wage, and well above what many of the jobs that keep North Meck running actually pay.</p>

<h2>Rent went one way. Wages went another.</h2>

<p>A decade ago, the median rent in Mecklenburg County was about $1,262 a month in today's dollars. By 2024 it had reached $1,762, representing a 40% jump after adjusting for inflation. Over the same stretch, pay for many local roles barely moved.</p>

<blockquote class="post-quote">A minimum-wage worker would need 175 hours of work a week to afford a one-bedroom. A week only has 168.</blockquote>

<h2>Who feels it first</h2>

<p>When rent outpaces wages, the squeeze does not land evenly. Households with the lowest incomes and renters of color, after generations of housing discrimination, are pushed hardest. Today roughly half of all renter households in the county are "cost-burdened," meaning housing eats up more than 30% of what they earn.</p>

<h2>What this means</h2>

<p>The gap between rent and wages is not abstract, as it shows up as longer commutes, doubled-up households, and a rising tide of eviction filings. Understanding the size of the gap is the first step toward closing it, and it is why every figure on this site links back to its public source.</p>
