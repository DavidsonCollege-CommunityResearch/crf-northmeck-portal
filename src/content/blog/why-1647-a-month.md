<p class="post-lead">Ask anyone renting in Cornelius, Davidson, or Huntersville and you'll hear a version of the same story: the rent keeps climbing, and the paycheck doesn't. Our own numbers back them up.</p>

<h2>The number that started it</h2>

<p>For a one-bedroom apartment at fair market rent in the Charlotte-Concord-Gastonia region, a full-time worker needs to earn <strong>$35.08 an hour</strong> to spend no more than 30% of their income on housing.</p>

<p>That's more than four times the federal minimum wage. Of the eight local occupations we tracked, only one clears that bar.</p>

<h2>And locally, rent went up fast</h2>

<p>Zoom into Davidson, Cornelius, and Huntersville specifically, and the trend is just as sharp. Average across all three towns, median rent rose from about <strong>$1,241 a month in 2018 to $1,736 in 2024</strong> — a 40% jump in just six years.</p>

<p>The three towns didn't move at exactly the same pace. Huntersville's median rent climbed from $1,288 to $1,834 (+42%). Cornelius went from $1,222 to $1,722 (+41%). Davidson, starting from a similar point at $1,213, reached $1,652 by 2024 (+36%) — still a steep rise, even as the smallest of the three.</p>

<h2>Who feels it first</h2>

<p>By 2024, cost burden — spending more than 30% of income on housing — had become the norm for renters across all three towns, not the exception.</p>

<h2>What this means</h2>

<p>The gap between rent and income is not abstract — it shows up in the rent-to-income ratios climbing in every one of our three towns, and in the growing share of neighbors who are cost-burdened. Understanding the size of the gap is the first step toward closing it, which is why every figure on this site links back to its public source.</p>
